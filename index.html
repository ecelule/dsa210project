Exploring how development indicators affect youth mortality using statistics and machine learning

About the Project

This project investigates why some regions experience higher youth mortality rates (ages 5–24) than others.

To answer this question, I combined:

  • Statistical analysis
  • Hypothesis testing
  • Machine learning models
  • Time-based forecasting

The dataset combines: UNICEF mortality data, HDI indicators, health spending, education spending, and regional information.

Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)

Initial analysis showed strong relationships between development indicators and mortality.

  • HDI, health spending, and education spending are strongly positively correlated
  • All development indicators are negatively correlated with mortality
  • Higher development generally corresponds to lower youth mortality

However, the variables are also highly correlated with each other, creating a multicollinearity issue.

Hypothesis Testing & Statistical Interpretation

OLS regression was used to better understand the underlying relationships.

  • R² ≈ 0.78 → strong explanatory power
  • Health spending has a strong negative effect on mortality
  • Education spending shows multicollinearity-related behavior
  • HDI becomes statistically weaker after adding controls
  • Regional effects remain significant

This suggests that development indicators matter strongly, but their effects overlap substantially.

Machine Learning Models

Several predictive models were tested:

  • Linear Regression
  • KNN Regression
  • Baseline Mean Model

The best performing model was: KNN Regression

  • Highest R²
  • Lowest RMSE
  • Better handling of non-linear patterns

Time-Based Forecasting

The project also tested whether mortality trends could be predicted over time.

  • Models were trained on earlier years
  • Tested on more recent years
  • General mortality trends were captured successfully
  • Year-to-year fluctuations remained difficult

Main insight: Predicting long-term levels is easier than predicting short-term changes.

Limitations

  • Strong multicollinearity between predictors
  • Regional averages hide country-level variation
  • Limited time depth for forecasting
  • Some mortality spikes are difficult to predict accurately
